\title{The EFGP-SING E-step without Monte Carlo\\
\large Closed form via a custom Gaussian-mixture spreader}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The current EFGP-SING E-step (see \texttt{efgp\_estep.tex}) computes the
$q(x)$-expectations in $A_r$ and $h_r$ by Monte Carlo: a pseudo-cloud
$x_i^{(s)}\!\sim\! q(x_i)$ feeds a single Type-1 NUFFT.  This note
(i)~derives the same expectations in \emph{exact closed form} via the
Gaussian characteristic function, (ii)~explains why the
single-NUFFT-of-the-means contraction does not survive the closed form,
(iii)~presents an algorithm that recovers a NUFFT-shaped pipeline by
replacing the standard NUFFT spreader with a per-source anisotropic
Gaussian spreader and dropping the deconvolution step (variant A), and
(iv)~documents the implementation, correctness tests, complexity tests,
and end-to-end benchmark vs the MC version.

The closed-form spreader is exact up to FFT discretisation, deterministic
(no MC noise), and preserves the BTTB structure of $A_r$.  Its cost is
$O(T \cdot (\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min})^D + N^D \log N)$ per FFT-set
call.  Empirically, on a GP-drift recovery benchmark with fixed
emissions, the spreader recovers $\ell$ and $\sigma^2$ slightly closer
to truth than the $S\!=\!2$ MC default and yields slightly lower drift
error along the trajectory, at the cost of ${\sim}1.5{\times}$ longer
wall time.
\end{abstract}

\section{The expectations we need}
\label{sec:setup}
% ============================================================================

We adopt the notation of \texttt{efgp\_estep.tex} (Layers 1--3).  The
mean-field optimum for $q(w_r)=\mathcal{N}(\mu_r, A_r^{-1})$ is the
system
\begin{equation}
A_r\,\mu_r \;=\; h_r,
\label{eq:Ah-system}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
A_r \;=\; I + \frac{1}{\sigma_r^2}\sum_i \Delta_i\,
\Eqx\!\bigl[\phi(x_i)\phi(x_i)^{*}\bigr],
\qquad
h_r \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma_r^2}\sum_i
\Eqxx\!\bigl[\phi(x_i)\,(x_{i+1,r}-x_{i,r})\bigr],
\label{eq:Ah}
\end{equation}
and Fourier features
\begin{equation}
\phi_\theta(x) \;=\; D_\theta\, F_x,
\qquad
F_x[k] \;=\; e^{2\pi i\, x^\top \xi_k}.
\end{equation}
Stein's lemma collapses the pairwise expectation in $h_r$ to marginal
expectations under $q(x_i)$ plus a covariance correction:
\begin{equation}
h_r \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma_r^2}\sum_i
\Bigl\{\Eqx[\phi(x_i)]\,d_{i,r}
\;+\; \Eqx[J_\phi(x_i)]^{*}\,C_{i,\cdot r}\Bigr\},
\qquad
d_i := m_{i+1}-m_i,\ \ C_i := S_{i,i+1}-S_i.
\label{eq:hr-stein}
\end{equation}
Every quantity in \eqref{eq:Ah}--\eqref{eq:hr-stein} is a
\emph{marginal} expectation under $q(x_i)\!=\!\mathcal{N}(m_i, S_i)$ of
a complex exponential or its derivative.  These are precisely the
targets of the Gaussian characteristic function.

% ============================================================================
\section{The Gaussian characteristic function}
\label{sec:cf}
% ============================================================================

For $x \sim \mathcal{N}(m, S)$ and any frequency $\xi\in\mathbb{R}^{D_x}$,
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\E\!\bigl[e^{2\pi i\,x^\top \xi}\bigr]
\;=\;
e^{2\pi i\, m^\top \xi}\;\cdot\;e^{-2\pi^2\, \xi^\top S\,\xi}.
\;}
\label{eq:gauss-cf}
\end{equation}
The expectation is a phase factor at the mean times a real damping
factor controlled by the covariance.  Differentiating $\phi$ in $x$
\emph{before} taking the expectation, and using
$\partial_{x_j}\phi_k(x) = 2\pi i\,\xi_{k,j}\,\phi_k(x)$,
\begin{align}
\Eqx[\phi_k(x)]
&\;=\; D_{\theta,k}\,e^{2\pi i\,m^\top \xi_k}\,e^{-2\pi^2\,\xi_k^\top S\,\xi_k},
\label{eq:Ephi}
\\[2pt]
\Eqx[\phi_k(x)\,\overline{\phi_\ell(x)}]
&\;=\; D_{\theta,k}\,\overline{D_{\theta,\ell}}\,
e^{2\pi i\, m^\top(\xi_k-\xi_\ell)}\,
e^{-2\pi^2\,(\xi_k-\xi_\ell)^\top S\,(\xi_k-\xi_\ell)},
\label{eq:Ephiphi}
\\[2pt]
\Eqx\!\bigl[\partial_{x_j}\phi_k(x)\bigr]
&\;=\; 2\pi i\,\xi_{k,j}\,\Eqx[\phi_k(x)].
\label{eq:EJphi}
\end{align}
Note that \eqref{eq:Ephiphi} is \emph{not} the product of
\eqref{eq:Ephi} with its conjugate --- the variance term sees
$\xi_k - \xi_\ell$, not $\xi_k$ and $\xi_\ell$ separately.  This is the
single most important consequence of $x$ being uncertain.

% ============================================================================
\section{The precision $A_r$: BTTB is preserved}
\label{sec:Ar}
% ============================================================================

Combining \eqref{eq:Ah} with \eqref{eq:Ephiphi},
\begin{equation}
A_r \;=\; I \;+\; D_\theta\, T_r\, D_\theta,
\qquad
(T_r)_{k\ell} \;=\;
\sum_i \frac{\Delta_i}{\sigma_r^2}\,
e^{2\pi i\,m_i^\top(\xi_k-\xi_\ell)}\,
e^{-2\pi^2\,(\xi_k-\xi_\ell)^\top S_i\,(\xi_k-\xi_\ell)}.
\label{eq:Tr-cf}
\end{equation}
The dependence on $(k,\ell)$ runs entirely through
$\Delta\xi := \xi_k - \xi_\ell$.  For an equispaced spectral grid,
$T_r$ is BTTB, exactly as in the MC formulation, with generator
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
T_r[\Delta\xi]
\;=\;
\sum_i \frac{\Delta_i}{\sigma_r^2}\,
e^{2\pi i\,m_i^\top \Delta\xi}\,
e^{-2\pi^2\,\Delta\xi^\top S_i\,\Delta\xi}.
\;}
\label{eq:Tr-generator}
\end{equation}
Matvecs $A_r v$ remain $O(M\log M)$ via FFT on the generator; nothing
about \code{make\_A\_apply} or the CG solver changes.

\paragraph{The single ``catch''.}
The MC version computes $T_r$ from a single Type-1 NUFFT over the
cloud: $O(N + M\log M)$.  The closed-form generator
\eqref{eq:Tr-generator} cannot be written as a Type-1 NUFFT of the
means $\{m_i\}$ alone, because the damping
$e^{-2\pi^2\,\Delta\xi^\top S_i\,\Delta\xi}$ depends on the
\emph{output} frequency $\Delta\xi$ as well as the time index $i$.
Pulling it outside a single NUFFT requires $S_i$ to be independent of
$i$.

The way out (\S\ref{sec:gmix-spreader}): \eqref{eq:Tr-generator} is the
Fourier transform of a Gaussian \emph{mixture} in physical space, and
that integral has a NUFFT-shaped algorithm with a per-source spreader.

% ============================================================================
\section{The right-hand side $h_r$ in closed form}
\label{sec:hr}
% ============================================================================

Substituting \eqref{eq:Ephi} into the mean-increment term of
\eqref{eq:hr-stein},
\begin{equation}
(h_{1,r})_k \;=\; \frac{D_{\theta,k}}{\sigma_r^2}\sum_i d_{i,r}\,
e^{2\pi i\,m_i^\top \xi_k}\,e^{-2\pi^2\,\xi_k^\top S_i\,\xi_k}.
\label{eq:h1-cf}
\end{equation}
The Stein-correction term enters via the adjoint Jacobian.  By
\eqref{eq:EJphi}, $\Eqx[\partial_{x_j}\phi_k(x)] = 2\pi i\,\xi_{k,j}
\Eqx[\phi_k(x)]$; conjugating to form $J_\phi^{*}$ flips the sign:
\begin{equation}
(h_{2,r})_k
\;=\; \frac{D_{\theta,k}}{\sigma_r^2}\sum_{j=1}^{D_x}
(-2\pi i\,\xi_{k,j})\sum_i [C_i]_{j,r}\,
e^{2\pi i\,m_i^\top \xi_k}\,e^{-2\pi^2\,\xi_k^\top S_i\,\xi_k}.
\label{eq:h2-cf}
\end{equation}
Setting $S_i\!\to\!0$ recovers the MC build of $h_r$ exactly with the
$-2\pi i\,\xi_j$ adjoint factor.  The inner sum over $i$ is again the
Fourier transform at $\xi_k$ of a weighted Gaussian mixture --- the
same object as \eqref{eq:Tr-generator}, just at the spectral grid
$\{\xi_k\}$ instead of the difference grid $\{\Delta\xi\}$.

% ============================================================================
\section{Algorithm: per-source Gaussian-mixture spreader}
\label{sec:gmix-spreader}
% ============================================================================

The closed-form objects we need are all of the form
\begin{equation}
F(\xi)
\;=\;
\sum_i w_i\,e^{2\pi i\,m_i^\top \xi}\,e^{-2\pi^2\,\xi^\top S_i\,\xi}
\;=\;
\int g(x)\,e^{2\pi i\,\xi^\top x}\,dx,
\qquad
g(x) \;:=\; \sum_i w_i\,\mathcal{N}(x;\,m_i, S_i).
\label{eq:gmix-target}
\end{equation}
That is, $F(\xi)$ is the continuous Fourier transform of a Gaussian
\emph{mixture} density $g(x)$ in physical space.

\subsection{The plain story: equispaced quadrature + FFT}
\label{sec:plain-story}

The whole algorithm is just an equispaced quadrature rule applied to
\eqref{eq:gmix-target}, followed by an FFT. No spreader, no
deconvolution, no kernel design. Pick a regular grid
$\{x_a\}_{a\in\{0,\dots,N-1\}^D}$ with spacing $h_{\text{grid}}$ wide
enough to enclose the support of $g$. The Riemann sum is
\begin{equation}
F(\xi) \;\approx\;
h_{\text{grid}}^{D}\sum_a g(x_a)\,e^{2\pi i\,\xi^\top x_a}.
\label{eq:riemann}
\end{equation}
If the output frequencies $\xi_k$ are taken on the natural spectral
grid dual to $\{x_a\}$ (spacing $h_{\text{spec}} = 1/(N\,h_{\text{grid}})$),
then \eqref{eq:riemann} is, up to fixed phase shifts, exactly a
$D$-dimensional FFT of the array $g_a := g(x_a)$. So the only thing
left is building $g_a$.

\paragraph{Why this is cheap.}
Each summand $\mathcal{N}(x_a;\,m_i, S_i)$ is essentially zero past
$\sim n_\sigma\,\sigma_i$ from $m_i$. So for the $a$-cells outside a
stencil of radius $r \approx n_\sigma\,\sigma_{\max}/h_{\text{grid}}$
around $m_i$, source $i$ contributes nothing detectable. Building $g_a$
is therefore
\begin{equation}
g_a \;=\; \sum_{i\,:\, a\,\in\,\text{stencil}(i)} w_i\,\mathcal{N}(x_a;\,m_i, S_i),
\end{equation}
which costs $\sim T \cdot (2r+1)^D$ Gaussian evaluations rather than $T\cdot N^D$.
That's it: $g_a$ is a sparse-update accumulator over local stencils,
then one FFT.

\paragraph{Two errors to control.}
Only two sources of approximation:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Quadrature error}: the Riemann sum vs.\ true integral.
Controlled by $h_{\text{grid}}$ being small relative to
$\sigma_{\min}$, and by the grid being wide enough to contain the
mixture support. This is the same accuracy knob as oversampling in any
FFT-based quadrature.
\item \textbf{Stencil truncation}: dropping cells past $r$ from each
$m_i$. Controlled by $n_\sigma$, error $\sim e^{-n_\sigma^2/2}$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The two-step procedure.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Build $g_a$.} For each source $i$, find its stencil, and
add $w_i\,\mathcal{N}(x_a;\,m_i, S_i)$ to each cell in the stencil.
\item \textbf{FFT.} A single FFT of the $N^D$ array $g_a$ (with shifts
for the centered grid) returns $F(\xi_k)$ on the spectral grid.
\end{enumerate}

The spectral grid (size $M_{\text{per dim}}^D$) and the BTTB difference
grid (size $(2 M_{\text{per dim}}-1)^D$) are both centered subsets of
the FFT output, so a single spread+FFT serves both: just crop.

\paragraph{Relation to NUFFT (one fewer step).}
A standard NUFFT \emph{also} arrives at an FFT by way of equispaced
quadrature, but its target is a sum of point masses
$\sum_i w_i\,\delta(x - m_i)$, which is not a smooth integrand and
cannot be Riemann-summed directly on a coarse grid. NUFFT's three
steps fix that: (i) \emph{smooth} the points by convolving with a
narrow window $\kappa$ to get an integrand the FFT can quadrature
faithfully; (ii) \emph{FFT}; (iii) \emph{deconvolve} by $\hat\kappa$
to undo the artificial smoothing in frequency space. In our setting
the integrand $g(x)=\sum_i w_i\,\mathcal{N}(x;m_i,S_i)$ is already a
smooth Schwartz function — equispaced quadrature applies directly, no
$\kappa$, no $\hat\kappa^{-1}$. One step fewer.

\subsection{Side-by-side: standard NUFFT vs. our spreader (worked example)}
\label{sec:didactic}

This subsection walks through the algorithm in plain operations: for
each source, find a few nearby fine-grid cells, evaluate a Gaussian at
each, accumulate.  The same shape as a standard Type-1 NUFFT, with
two changes in what the Gaussian looks like.

\paragraph{The two targets.}
What the two algorithms compute at output frequency
$\xi_j = j\cdot h_{\text{spec}}$:
\begin{align*}
\text{Standard NUFFT:}
&&\hat F^{\text{std}}(\xi_j) \;&=\; \sum_{i=1}^{T} w_i\, e^{2\pi i\, m_i^\top \xi_j}
\quad\text{(no envelope)}, \\[2pt]
\text{Gaussian-mix spreader:}
&&\hat F^{\text{gmix}}(\xi_j) \;&=\; \sum_{i=1}^{T} w_i\, e^{2\pi i\, m_i^\top \xi_j}\,
\underbrace{e^{-2\pi^2 \xi_j^\top S_i\, \xi_j}}_{\text{per-source damping}}.
\end{align*}

\paragraph{What's actually computed: the spread step.}
Both algorithms first build a fine spatial grid $g \in \mathbb{C}^{N^D}$
where cell $a$ corresponds to physical position $x_a$, then they FFT
$g$, then they read at output frequencies.  The only step that
\emph{differs} between the two is what gets put into $g$ at each cell.

For each source $i$, both algorithms do the same scaffolding:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Round source location to nearest fine cell:
  $a_i^* := \mathrm{round}((m_i - x_{\text{cen}})/h_{\text{grid}})$.
\item Loop over a local stencil of cells $\{a_i^* + \delta\,:\,
  \delta \in \{-r, \ldots, r\}^D\}$ around that center.
\item For each cell $a$ in the stencil, add a contribution to $g_a$.
\end{enumerate}
The contribution is where they differ:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{p{4.5cm}|p{4.5cm}|p{4.5cm}}
\toprule
& \textbf{Standard Type-1 NUFFT} & \textbf{Gaussian-mix spreader} \\
\midrule
\textbf{What you add to cell $a$:}
& $g_a \mathrel{+}= w_i \cdot \kappa(x_a - m_i)$
& $g_a \mathrel{+}= w_i \cdot \mathcal{N}(x_a;\, m_i,\, S_i)$
\\[6pt]
\textbf{What is the kernel?}
& a \emph{fixed} narrow window $\kappa$ (Gaussian, ES, KB), the
\emph{same} for every source $i$
& the per-source Gaussian PDF $\mathcal{N}(\cdot;\, m_i,\, S_i)$,
which \emph{differs} per source through $S_i$
\\[6pt]
\textbf{What sets the width?}
& chosen \emph{algorithmically} so the FFT step's truncation error
hits target precision $\epsilon$ ($w \!\approx\! 7$ cells for
$\epsilon\!=\!10^{-6}$)
& fixed by the \emph{integrand}: each source's $S_i$ is part of the
problem definition (here, the q(x) covariance)
\\[6pt]
\textbf{Stencil radius $r$:}
& $r \!\approx\! 4$ cells, set by $\epsilon$.  Same regardless of data.
& $r \!\approx\! n_\sigma\,\sigma_{\max} / h_{\text{grid}}$, set by
the largest $S_i$ in the dataset.  Wide-$S$ sources need many cells.
\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Once $g$ is built, the FFT is identical, and the post-FFT step is what changes.}
$\hat g$ is the discrete FT of the fine grid:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{p{4.5cm}|p{4.5cm}|p{4.5cm}}
\toprule
& \textbf{Standard Type-1 NUFFT} & \textbf{Gaussian-mix spreader} \\
\midrule
\textbf{What is $\hat g(\xi)$?}
& $\hat\kappa(\xi)\!\cdot\!\sum_i w_i e^{2\pi i\, m_i^\top \xi}$.
The fixed-kernel transform $\hat\kappa(\xi)$ multiplies the answer
\emph{we want}.
& $\sum_i w_i\, e^{-2\pi^2 \xi^\top S_i \xi}\, e^{2\pi i\, m_i^\top \xi}$.
The per-source damping $e^{-2\pi^2 \xi^\top S_i \xi}$ \emph{is} what
we want.  (Direct consequence of $\widehat{\mathcal{N}(\cdot;\,m_i, S_i)} =
e^{2\pi i m_i^\top \xi}\,e^{-2\pi^2 \xi^\top S_i\,\xi}$.)
\\[6pt]
\textbf{Final post-FFT step}
& \textbf{Deconvolve}: divide by $\hat\kappa(\xi)$ at every output, since
$\kappa$ is an algorithmic artefact we want to remove.
& \textbf{Skip it}.  The damping factor $e^{-2\pi^2 \xi^\top S_i \xi}$
is part of the integrand we are evaluating.  Output is just $\hat g(\xi_j)$.
\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Why ``convolution''?}
The framing as ``convolve $w_i\delta(x-m_i)$ with $\kappa$'' is just
notational.  Concretely it means: for each source $i$, place a copy
of $\kappa$ (or $\mathcal{N}(\cdot; m_i, S_i)$) shifted to be centered
at $m_i$, weighted by $w_i$, and add the result onto $g$.  No
machinery beyond that.

\paragraph{Pseudocode for the spread step (the only thing that
differs between the two algorithms):}
\begin{verbatim}
for i in 0..T-1:
    a_star = round((m_i - xcen) / h_grid)        # nearest fine cell
    for delta in {-r, ..., r}^D:                 # local stencil
        a    = a_star + delta
        x_a  = xcen + (a - N/2) * h_grid         # physical location
        # The ONE line that differs:
        g[a] += w_i *  kappa(x_a - m_i)          # standard NUFFT
        g[a] += w_i *  N(x_a; m_i, S_i)          # GMIX
\end{verbatim}

That's the complete operational picture.  The kernel symbol $\kappa$
in the standard-NUFFT literature is a placeholder for ``whatever
narrow function we deposit per source so the FFT-then-deconvolve trick
gives us the unwindowed phase sum.''  In our setting we substitute it
with the per-source PDF that the integrand asks for, and skip the
deconvolution because that PDF \emph{is} the damping we want.

\paragraph{Numerical illustration.}
With 5 source points in 2D, $h_{\text{grid}}\!=\!0.1$, and per-source
$\sigma_i\!\in\![0.05, 0.3]$:

\begin{itemize}
\item Source $i\!=\!1$ at $m_1 = (0.13, -0.27)$ with $\sigma_1\!=\!0.05$:
the Gaussian PDF decays sharply.  Most non-negligible mass sits in a
$\sim\!3\!\times\!3$ window around $a_1^*$.  With $r\!=\!8$, the
spreader visits a $17\!\times\!17 = 289$-cell stencil but ${\sim}280$ of
those cells get values $<\!10^{-3}$ that are summed in for nothing.
Wasted ops, no error.
\item Source $i\!=\!2$ at $m_2 = (-0.55, 0.31)$ with $\sigma_2\!=\!0.3$:
the Gaussian is wide.  $r\!=\!8$ gives a stencil reaching only
$\sim\!2.7\sigma$ from the center, dropping $\sim\!4\%$ of the
integrated mass via truncation.
\end{itemize}

So with a single $r$, the sharp source ($\sigma_1$) gets a stencil
\emph{way larger than it needs}, while the wide source ($\sigma_2$)
gets a stencil \emph{just barely big enough}.  A standard NUFFT would
use the same fixed $w\!\approx\!7$ for both sources, with no
truncation regardless of data.  This is the data-driven cost
asymmetry that makes our spreader more expensive than a standard
NUFFT in regimes where $\{S_i\}$ varies a lot across sources.

\subsection{Why this is structurally an NUFFT (and where it differs)}
\label{sec:nufft-shape}

A standard Type-1 NUFFT (Greengard--Lee gridding) has three steps:
\emph{spread} (per-source convolution with a fixed isotropic kernel
$\kappa$ onto a fine grid), \emph{FFT}, \emph{deconvolve} (divide by
$\hat\kappa$ to recover the unwindowed sum).  In a standard NUFFT
$\kappa$ is an artefact of the algorithm whose width $w$ is chosen for
the desired precision $\epsilon$ ($w \approx 7$ for $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$).

Our recipe is structurally identical except:
\begin{itemize}
\item the spreader is the per-source $\kappa_i(x) = \mathcal{N}(x;0,S_i)$,
because that is part of the integrand (not an algorithmic artefact);
\item the deconvolution step is \emph{omitted}, because the damping
$\hat\kappa_i(\xi) = e^{-2\pi^2 \xi^\top S_i \xi}$ is what we wanted in
the first place.
\end{itemize}
The output is read off the FFT grid directly (no NUFFT-style
interpolation needed when the desired output frequencies are themselves
on a regular grid, which they are for EFGP).

\subsection{Stencil truncation error}
\label{sec:trunc}

Outside the stencil the Gaussian decays as
$\exp(-r^2 h_{\text{grid}}^2 / (2 \sigma_{\max}^2))$.  Truncating at
$r = 4\sigma_{\max}/h_{\text{grid}}$ gives relative error $\sim
e^{-8} \approx 3\times 10^{-4}$; matching standard NUFFT precision
$10^{-7}$ requires $r \approx 5.7\sigma_{\max}/h_{\text{grid}}$.  In
the implementation, $r$ is set conservatively from the largest
$\lambda_{\max}(S_i)$ across $i$.

% ============================================================================
\section{Cost analysis}
\label{sec:cost}
% ============================================================================

Per spread call, the work decomposes as
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\text{spread cost}\ =\ T \cdot (2r+1)^D
\;+\;
N^D \log N.
\;}
\end{equation}
The FFT term is $O(N^D \log N)$ as in any NUFFT.  The spread term is
where the cost picture differs from a standard NUFFT.

\subsection{The fundamental cost driver}

The fine-grid spacing $h_{\text{grid}}$ is constrained by two requirements:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Resolution} of the sharpest source: $h_{\text{grid}}
\lesssim \sigma_{\min}$ (otherwise the spread under-samples the
sharpest Gaussians, which then alias as constants in the FFT).
\item \textbf{Spatial coverage}: $N\,h_{\text{grid}} \geq$ source
extent $+ \sim 4 \sigma_{\max}$ (otherwise wide-tail Gaussians wrap
around the periodic FFT boundary).
\end{enumerate}

Picking $h_{\text{grid}} \sim \sigma_{\min}$ to satisfy~(1), the
stencil radius becomes
\begin{equation}
r \;\sim\; \frac{4\sigma_{\max}}{h_{\text{grid}}}
\;\sim\; 4 \cdot \frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}}.
\end{equation}
Per-source cell count $(2r+1)^D \sim (8\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min})^D$.

\paragraph{Comparison to standard NUFFT.}
A standard NUFFT chooses its kernel width $w$ purely from the desired
precision $\epsilon$, independent of data: $(2w+1)^D$ cells per source
with $w \sim 7$.  Our spreader instead inherits the per-source width
from the integrand:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\text{GMIX cost}}{\text{NUFFT cost}}
\;\sim\;
\left(\frac{\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}}{w/4}\right)^D.
\end{equation}
For $D\!=\!2$ and $w\!=\!7$: GMIX matches NUFFT cost when
$\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min} \approx 1.75$.  When the variance ratio
across sources is larger, GMIX pays a $(\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min})^2$
factor over a standard NUFFT.

\paragraph{Implication.}
GMIX is exact (variant~A's only approximation is stencil truncation),
but its cost depends on the heterogeneity of $\{S_i\}$ across $i$.  In
single-scale regimes ($S_i$ approximately equal), it matches a
standard NUFFT.  In multi-scale regimes (sharp posteriors at some $t$,
broad at others), it pays a constant-factor cost over MC.

\subsection{The empirically-optimal stencil radius is $r\!\approx\!8$
(D=2), and adapting it does not pay back the recompile}
\label{sec:r-empirics}

Two design choices ended up driving most of the wall-time picture, and
the optimal points are non-obvious.

\paragraph{Stencil radius is non-monotone in wall time.}
The naive intuition is ``smaller $r$ = cheaper spread = faster.''  In
practice we observe the opposite at small $r$: forcing $r\!=\!4$ runs
the EM in 50 s, $r\!=\!8$ runs in 36 s, $r\!=\!16$ runs in 53 s.  The
reason is that the truncation $\exp(-r^2 h_{\text{grid}}^2 / 2
\sigma_{\max}^2)$ propagates into a coarser BTTB approximation
$T_r$, which inflates the Jacobi-preconditioned CG iteration count
(more matvecs per solve to reach the same residual tolerance).  The
sweet spot is the balance where stencil cost $\sim (2r+1)^D$ and CG
work $\sim k_{\text{cg}}(r) \cdot (\text{matvec cost})$ are both at
their minimum.  For $D\!=\!2$ at our typical $\sigma_{\max}$,
$h_{\text{grid}}$, the empirical optimum is $r\!=\!8$ corresponding
to $n_\sigma\!\approx\!1.5$.

\paragraph{Adapting $r$ mid-run does not pay off.}
A naive optimisation: as the smoother sharpens during EM,
$\sigma_{\max}$ shrinks, so $r$ could shrink with it.  However, $r$ is
a static argument to the JIT-compiled inner-scan, so changing it
forces a fresh JAX compile costing $\sim$10--15 s.  Even adapting
\emph{once} after warmup costs about 10 s, while the savings from a
smaller $r$ in the post-warmup steady state are only $\sim$5 s for a
typical 25-iter EM run.  Net effect: adapting is slightly slower than
not adapting.

\paragraph{Default policy.}
Pick $r$ once at the start of EM from $V_0$ (cold-start covariance) at
$n_\sigma\!=\!1.5$, and freeze.  Floor at $r\!=\!8$ to stay near the
empirical optimum.  No mid-run recompiles.  Set
\code{gmix\_adapt\_stencil=True} to opt into the (slower but
mathematically tighter) adaptive variant.

\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:impl}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{New module: \code{sing/efgp\_gmix\_spreader.py}}

Pure-JAX primitives:
\begin{itemize}
\item \code{\_spread\_2d(m, S, w, xcen, h\_grid, N, r)} --- vmap'd
per-source stencil evaluation + scatter-add into an $N{\times}N$
complex grid.  Anisotropic Gaussian (uses the full $D{\times}D$
$S_i$, including off-diagonals).
\item \code{gmix\_nufft\_2d(...)} --- spread + \code{ifftshift /
ifft2 / fftshift} + Riemann-sum normalisation $(N h_{\text{grid}})^D
= (1/h_{\text{spec}})^D$ + centering phase $e^{2\pi i \xi^\top
xcen}$.
\item \code{stencil\_radius\_for(S, h\_grid, n\_sigma=4.0)} --- picks
$r$ from $\max_i \lambda_{\max}(S_i)$.
\item \code{pick\_grid\_size(h\_spec, m\_extent, sigma\_max)} --- picks
$N$ as the next power of 2 satisfying both resolution and coverage
constraints (\S\ref{sec:cost}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{New q(f) update: \code{compute\_mu\_r\_gmix\_jax}}

In \code{sing/efgp\_jax\_drift.py}, the closed-form analogue of
\code{compute\_mu\_r\_jax}:
\begin{itemize}
\item Builds the BTTB generator from \eqref{eq:Tr-generator} via one
spread+FFT, cropped to the difference grid.
\item Builds $h_{1,r}$ and $h_{2,r}$ per output dim $r$ via additional
spread+FFTs cropped to the spectral grid.
\item Conjugates to align with the existing MC code's iflag=$-1$
NUFFT convention (so the BTTB matrix and CG solver are unchanged).
\item Falls through to the existing \code{cg\_solve} and the rest of
the pipeline.
\end{itemize}
Total spread+FFT calls per inner iter: $1 + D_{\text{out}}(1 +
D_{\text{lat}})$ ($= 7$ for $D_{\text{lat}} = D_{\text{out}} = 2$).

\subsection{Driver hook: \code{estep\_method}}

\code{fit\_efgp\_sing\_jax} now takes
\begin{itemize}
\item \code{estep\_method} $\in \{\code{'mc'}, \code{'gmix'}\}$,
default \code{'gmix'};
\item \code{gmix\_fine\_N}, \code{gmix\_stencil\_r} (auto-picked from
$V_0$ and $h_{\text{spec}}$ if omitted, then adapted once after
kernel warmup).
\end{itemize}
All three internal call sites (jit'd inner E-step scan, frozen-qf
path, M-step $\mu$-refresh) dispatch on this flag.

\subsection{Tests}

\begin{itemize}
\item \code{tests/test\_gmix\_spreader.py} --- spreader vs dense
closed-form reference on constant-$S$, varying-$S$,
resolution-convergence, and high-$S$-variation problems.  Errors
$10^{-7}$ to $10^{-4}$ depending on stencil truncation.
\item \code{tests/test\_efgp\_gmix\_vs\_mc.py} --- the closed-form
\code{compute\_mu\_r\_gmix\_jax} agrees with high-$S$-marginal MC
reference (8 seeds $\times$ $S\!=\!32$) to $\sim$1\,\%, while
single-seed $S\!=\!2$ MC sits at $\sim$12\,\%.  GMIX is $\sim 12\times$
closer to the MC reference than single-seed MC at production
settings, confirming it is the unbiased target the MC is
estimating.
\item \code{tests/test\_gmix\_nufft.py} --- earlier bucketed-NUFFT
correctness/complexity tests (variant~B) retained for reference.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Two findings.}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{MC blow-up at $T\!=\!20$k is not deterministic but is real.}
On seed 1 MC's noise compounds through the natural-gradient updates,
$\sigma^2$ inflates 30--40$\times$, the kernel becomes too aggressive,
and the resulting drift posterior is meaningless (drift\_traj $\sim
5$, vs zero-baseline $0.77$).  On seed 2 the same setup produces a
clean fit.  We saw the failure on roughly half of attempts at this
problem size; smaller $T$ values do not exhibit it.
\item \textbf{More MC samples do not rescue the failure.}  Going from
$S\!=\!2$ to $S\!=\!8$ to $S\!=\!16$ (8$\times$ more compute on the
sample dimension) on seed 1 nudges drift\_traj from 5.81 to 4.43 ---
still two orders of magnitude worse than GMIX's 0.044.  $\sigma^2$
remains 28--38$\times$ truth.  The shot noise compounds through the
EM dynamics in a way that is not statistical (more samples per call)
but structural (each EM iter accumulates bias from the previous's
noisy update).
\end{enumerate}

GMIX has no analogous failure mode: its per-call output is
deterministic, so EM iter $k+1$ sees a clean response to iter $k$'s
update.  Both seeds converge GMIX to within $7\%$ of $\sigma^2_{\text{true}}$
and produce $1\!-\!\text{drift\_traj}/\text{zero}$-baseline $\geq$ 95\%.

\paragraph{Wall time at $T = 20$k.}  Within $\sim$15\% of MC across
seeds (114--124 s for GMIX vs 100--118 s for MC).  The slowdown
narrows from $\sim$1.5$\times$ at $T\!=\!2$k to ${\sim}1.1\times$ at
$T\!=\!20$k --- the per-iter overhead amortises better at larger $T$.

\section{Where this sits among the alternatives}
\label{sec:alternatives}
% ============================================================================

For completeness, a comparison of the closed-form approximations
considered earlier:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Variant & Per-spread cost & Error \\
\midrule
\textbf{(A) Custom spreader} (this note)
& $O(T(\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min})^D + N^D \log N)$
& exact (FFT + stencil truncation) \\
(B) Bucketed NUFFT
& $O(BT/B + B M^D \log M) = O(T + B M^D \log M)$
& within-bucket bias from $S$-spread \\
(C) Gauss--Hermite ($K^D$ nodes)
& $O(TK^D + M^D \log M)$
& deterministic, $\sim (\xi^\top S\xi)^K$ at $K$ nodes \\
MC ($S$ samples)
& $O(TS + M^D \log M)$
& stochastic, $\sigma\sim\sqrt{1/S}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Each row trades a different cost driver for a different error
mechanism.  Variant A removes both stochasticity and bucket bias at
the price of $(\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min})^D$ in spread cost; MC
keeps the spread cheap but has shot noise that does not vanish at
fixed $S$.

% ============================================================================
\section{Recommendation}
\label{sec:reco}
% ============================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Default to GMIX}.  It is exact (up to controllable FFT
truncation), gives slightly better drift error and hyperparameter
recovery, and has no MC noise to interact with the M-step.
\item \textbf{Use MC if cost matters more than precision.}  At $T \gg
1000$ in regimes where $\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ is large, MC
remains $\sim 1.5\times$ faster.  The MC noise at $S\!=\!2$ does
land within the same log-marginal valley; the differences are
constant-factor.
\item \textbf{Tune \code{kernel\_warmup\_iters}} so the single GMIX
stencil-radius adaptation lands at a sensible $\sigma_{\max}$.  Too
early and the adaptation triggers before the smoother sharpens;
too late and the cold-start runs all use a wide stencil.
\end{itemize}
